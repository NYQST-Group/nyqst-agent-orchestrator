# Commercial Real Estate Asset Management Workflows

## Executive Summary

This research document provides a comprehensive analysis of commercial real estate (CRE) asset management workflows, focusing on what asset managers actually DO after acquisition. Asset management sits between portfolio management (investor-facing) and property management (tenant-facing), serving as the market-facing strategic layer that drives value creation and operational excellence.

**Key Finding for 2026**: The commercial real estate sector is entering 2026 with renewed momentum, clearer visibility, and growing optimism. Organizations that operationalize AI inside their workflows are outpacing those who stay in pilot mode, with the "AI productivity gap" widening between early adopters and laggards. While 92% of CRE firms have started or plan to pilot AI initiatives, only 5% have achieved all their program goals.

## 1. OPERATIONAL MANAGEMENT

### 1.1 Property Management Oversight

#### Workflow Steps

1. **Select and Onboard Property Management Firm**
   - Evaluate PM firm capabilities and track record
   - Negotiate management agreement terms (typically 4-12% of gross monthly rent or $500-$5,000/month)
   - Set performance metrics and reporting requirements
   - Establish communication protocols and escalation procedures

2. **Set Strategic Direction**
   - Define operational standards and expectations
   - Establish budget parameters and approval thresholds
   - Set occupancy targets and leasing strategies
   - Align PM activities with asset business plan

3. **Monitor Daily Operations**
   - Review weekly/monthly PM reports
   - Track maintenance completion rates
   - Monitor vendor performance and contracts
   - Ensure compliance with building codes and regulations

4. **Conduct Regular Performance Reviews**
   - Quarterly business review meetings with PM
   - Assess performance against KPIs
   - Review financial performance vs. budget
   - Identify operational improvement opportunities

#### Documents & Data

- Property management agreements
- Weekly/monthly operational reports
- Vendor contracts and performance metrics
- Maintenance logs and work order history
- Compliance certificates and inspection reports
- Operating expense detail by category
- Utility consumption data

#### Key Responsibilities

Asset managers work in tandem with property managers, overseeing daily operations, managing lease agreements, and maintaining strong tenant relationships, with a focus on keeping properties well-maintained and operationally efficient while aligning with long-term strategic goals. The hierarchy typically works where portfolio management is investor-facing, asset management is market-facing, and property management is tenant-facing.

### 1.2 Service Charge Management

#### Workflow Steps

1. **Annual Service Charge Budgeting**
   - Prepare service charge budget by category
   - Calculate tenant apportionments by lease terms
   - Issue budget notices to tenants (typically 2-3 months before year-end)
   - Obtain tenant approvals where required by lease

2. **Monthly Reconciliation**
   - Review actual expenditures vs. budget
   - Process vendor invoices for service charge items
   - Calculate monthly tenant billings
   - Track collections and arrears

3. **Year-End Reconciliation**
   - Compile final actual costs by category
   - Calculate over/under charges per tenant
   - Prepare detailed reconciliation statements
   - Issue balancing charges or refunds
   - Handle tenant disputes and queries

4. **CAM Charge Administration**
   - Track Common Area Maintenance expenses
   - Apply caps and exclusions per lease terms
   - Calculate pro rata shares
   - Ensure GAAP compliance

#### Documents & Data

- Service charge budgets and forecasts
- Vendor invoices and payment records
- Tenant apportionment schedules
- Monthly reconciliation reports
- Year-end service charge certificates
- Lease abstracts showing service charge provisions
- CAM reconciliation statements

#### Technology Solutions (2026)

Property management software can automate CAM/Service charges/Outgoings effectively, ensuring everything is compliant. Systems like MRI Qube PM excel at service charges including budgets, caps, multiple schedules and apportionments, and reconciliations, tied to robust accounting and reporting.

### 1.3 Tenant Relations and Retention

#### Workflow Steps

1. **Proactive Tenant Engagement**
   - Schedule regular tenant meetings (quarterly minimum)
   - Conduct annual tenant satisfaction surveys
   - Establish responsive communication channels
   - Build relationships with tenant decision-makers

2. **Issue Resolution**
   - Track and respond to tenant requests promptly
   - Escalate critical issues to asset manager
   - Document all tenant interactions
   - Follow up to ensure satisfaction

3. **Retention Strategy Execution**
   - Identify tenants at risk 12-18 months before expiry
   - Understand tenant space and business needs
   - Prepare retention proposals and incentives
   - Negotiate lease renewals proactively

4. **Value-Add Services**
   - Provide tenant portal access for payments and requests
   - Offer property upgrades or improvements
   - Share market intelligence and insights
   - Support tenant business objectives

#### Documents & Data

- Tenant contact database
- Tenant satisfaction survey results
- Issue tracking logs and resolution times
- Lease expiry schedules
- Renewal proposal templates
- Tenant financial health indicators
- Communication history and meeting notes

#### Industry Benchmarks

Commercial and retail properties should aim for around 70% tenant retention rates, which is higher than residential properties. A single vacancy can lead to $100,000 to $200,000 in losses, trigger additional tenant departures, and introduce instability into a previously thriving property.

#### Key Retention Strategies

1. **Proactive Communication** - Regular communication with tenants is essential for building strong relationships and addressing concerns before they escalate
2. **Responsive Maintenance** - Timely and efficient handling of maintenance requests shows tenants that their comfort and satisfaction are priorities
3. **Lease Incentives** - Offering incentives like property upgrades or a rent discount for early lease renewal is a win-win for both parties
4. **Technology Integration** - Providing tenants with access to an online portal for rent payments, maintenance requests, and communication with property management can streamline operations and enhance the tenant experience

### 1.4 Lease Events Management (Breaks, Renewals, Rent Reviews)

#### Workflow Steps

1. **Lease Event Tracking**
   - Maintain comprehensive lease event calendar
   - Set automated reminders 12-18 months before events
   - Review lease clauses and rights/obligations
   - Prepare event analysis and strategy

2. **Rent Review Process**
   - Review rent review clauses (open market, indexed, fixed uplift)
   - Conduct market rent analysis and comparables
   - Prepare supporting evidence and documentation
   - Negotiate with tenant or appoint surveyors
   - Document agreed rent or proceed to determination
   - Update rent roll and financial models

3. **Lease Break Management**
   - Identify break clauses and conditions
   - Monitor tenant intentions 6-12 months before break date
   - Assess lease break conditions (payment up to date, vacant possession, etc.)
   - Evaluate retention vs. re-letting strategy
   - If break exercised: prepare re-letting strategy and void management plan

4. **Lease Renewal Process**
   - Review existing lease terms and tenant performance
   - Assess market conditions and rental values
   - Prepare heads of terms for renewal
   - Negotiate lease length, rent, incentives
   - Instruct solicitors for lease documentation
   - Complete lease renewal and update systems

#### Documents & Data

- Lease event diary/calendar
- Lease abstracts with key dates and clauses
- Market rent analysis and comparables
- Rent review memoranda
- Break notice templates and acknowledgements
- Heads of terms for renewals
- Legal completion documentation
- Updated rent rolls

#### Technology Solutions (2026)

Modern systems help property managers say goodbye to missed rent reviews and renewals, with automated reminders of lease events, invoice generating, sending & processing. Diary tools cover rent reviews, lease expiries and inspections, with maintenance managed as planned/reactive with contractor links. Advanced systems receive intelligent suggestions such as advice on rent review clauses embedded directly in workflows.

### 1.5 Void Management

#### Workflow Steps

1. **Void Preparation**
   - Conduct pre-vacation inspection
   - Review dilapidations obligations
   - Negotiate dilapidations settlement
   - Take possession and document condition
   - Commission condition survey

2. **Refurbishment Strategy**
   - Assess market requirements and tenant demand
   - Determine scope of works and budget
   - Obtain planning permissions if required
   - Tender and appoint contractors
   - Project manage refurbishment works

3. **Re-letting Campaign**
   - Appoint letting agents
   - Prepare marketing materials and virtual tours
   - Set asking rent and incentive package
   - Market to target tenant sectors
   - Conduct viewings and negotiations
   - Review offers and select preferred tenant

4. **Void Cost Management**
   - Track holding costs (rates, utilities, security)
   - Minimize non-essential expenditure
   - Consider short-term uses or pop-up tenants
   - Monitor void period against budget assumptions

#### Documents & Data

- Dilapidations schedules and settlements
- Condition surveys and photo documentation
- Refurbishment budgets and contracts
- Contractor quotes and project timelines
- Marketing materials and brochures
- Void cost tracking reports
- Letting agent activity reports
- Offer summaries and tenant covenant assessments

#### Financial Impact

Asset managers must minimize vacancy cycles as they directly impact NOI and property values. Commercial properties typically experience vacancy costs of $100,000-$200,000 per void, making efficient void management a critical value preservation activity.

## 2. FINANCIAL MANAGEMENT

### 2.1 Budgeting (Annual, CapEx, Business Plans)

#### 2.1.1 Annual Operating Budget

**Workflow Steps:**

1. **Budget Preparation (Q3/Q4)**
   - Review current year actuals and run-rate
   - Obtain property manager input on operating costs
   - Incorporate known lease events and changes
   - Model rental income by tenant and unit
   - Forecast vacancy and bad debt provisions
   - Estimate operating expenses by category
   - Include management fees and professional costs

2. **Budget Review and Approval**
   - Present budget to investment committee
   - Obtain investor/board approval
   - Finalize and distribute approved budget
   - Load budget into financial systems

3. **Budget Communication**
   - Brief property management team
   - Set spending authorities and approval limits
   - Establish variance monitoring procedures
   - Schedule monthly/quarterly budget reviews

**Documents & Data:**
- Annual operating budget template
- Prior year actuals and current year forecast
- Property manager cost estimates
- Rent roll with lease expiries and events
- Insurance quotations
- Utility rate projections
- Service charge apportionments

#### 2.1.2 Capital Expenditure (CapEx) Budget

**Workflow Steps:**

1. **CapEx Planning**
   - Conduct building condition assessment
   - Review asset business plan priorities
   - Identify value-add opportunities
   - Assess regulatory compliance requirements (ESG, building safety)
   - Develop multi-year CapEx plan

2. **Project Prioritization**
   - Rank projects by ROI and strategic importance
   - Consider tenant requirements and retention
   - Align with available capital and debt covenants
   - Create phased implementation plan

3. **CapEx Approval Process**
   - Prepare detailed project business cases
   - Obtain cost estimates and contractor quotes
   - Present to investment committee
   - Secure approval and funding

4. **CapEx Execution**
   - Appoint project managers and contractors
   - Monitor project costs vs. budget
   - Track project milestones and completion
   - Conduct post-completion reviews

**Documents & Data:**
- Building condition survey reports
- CapEx project pipeline and prioritization matrix
- Project business cases with ROI analysis
- Contractor quotes and cost estimates
- Project approval documentation
- CapEx tracking and variance reports
- Completion certificates and as-built drawings

**Best Practice Guidelines:**

A common rule of thumb is to set aside 1-2% of the property's value annually for CapEx, though this varies by property type and condition. When planned and executed strategically, CapEx initiatives materially shift an asset's performance trajectory, with each targeted improvement directly influencing rent potential, strengthening occupancy, and driving overall asset value.

#### 2.1.3 Asset Business Plans

**Workflow Steps:**

1. **Business Plan Development (At Acquisition and Annually)**
   - Review acquisition underwriting assumptions
   - Conduct market and competitive analysis
   - Define value creation strategy
   - Set 3-5 year financial targets
   - Identify key value drivers and risks
   - Develop implementation roadmap

2. **Strategic Initiatives**
   - Lease restructuring opportunities
   - CapEx and repositioning plans
   - Tenant mix optimization
   - Operational efficiency improvements
   - ESG and sustainability initiatives
   - Exit timing and strategy

3. **Business Plan Execution**
   - Translate plan into annual budgets and actions
   - Assign responsibilities and timelines
   - Monitor progress against milestones
   - Report to investors on plan execution

4. **Annual Business Plan Review**
   - Assess progress vs. original plan
   - Update assumptions based on market changes
   - Revise strategy and targets as needed
   - Reset priorities for coming year

**Documents & Data:**
- Asset business plan document (typically 20-40 pages)
- Market analysis and competitive positioning
- Financial projections (5-10 years)
- Capital plan and funding requirements
- Risk assessment and mitigation strategies
- KPI dashboard and tracking metrics
- Quarterly progress reports

**Core Functions:**

Asset management combines spreadsheet/analysis focused approaches with practical knowledge to execute on a business plan for each property. Plans and budgets are needed to establish disbursement/draw schedules, and business plans for commercial properties are typically established before acquisition but may be modified during the process.

### 2.2 Actual vs Budget Monitoring

#### Workflow Steps

1. **Monthly Variance Analysis**
   - Download actuals from accounting system
   - Compare actual vs. budget by line item
   - Calculate variances ($ and %)
   - Identify material variances (typically >10% or >$X threshold)
   - Investigate root causes of variances

2. **Variance Explanation and Commentary**
   - Document reasons for variances
   - Distinguish one-time vs. recurring variances
   - Assess impact on full-year forecast
   - Prepare management commentary

3. **Forecast Updates**
   - Update full-year forecast based on actuals
   - Revise assumptions for remaining months
   - Calculate revised year-end position
   - Assess impact on investment returns

4. **Reporting and Action**
   - Present variance analysis to stakeholders
   - Recommend corrective actions if needed
   - Adjust operational approach as required
   - Update financial models

#### Documents & Data

- Monthly actual vs. budget variance reports
- GL transaction detail for investigation
- Variance explanation commentary
- Full-year forecast updates
- Trend analysis charts
- Cash flow forecasts

#### Common Variance Categories

- **Rent variances**: Lease events, tenant defaults, timing of rent-free periods
- **Operating expense variances**: Utilities, repairs, service contracts
- **Vacancy costs**: Longer/shorter void periods than budgeted
- **One-time items**: Legal fees, professional costs, insurance claims
- **Timing differences**: Prepayments, accruals, invoice timing

#### Technology Challenges (2026)

Finance teams typically spend 3-5 days each month manually compiling variance reports in Excel, leaving limited time for actual analysis. Multiple financial systems create inefficiencies in budget tracking and variance analysis, often leading to delayed insights, increased reporting time, and missed optimization opportunities.

**Technical Challenges:**
- COA mismatches between budget categories and GL expense posting require extensive mapping work each month
- Timing and accrual gaps occur when budgets are monthly but actuals arrive unevenly (prepaids, annual renewals, late invoices)
- Systems struggle with data visualization, multi-entity reporting, automation capabilities, complex budget structures and large data volumes
- Manual export/import creates time lag, and by the time the CFO sees the variance report, decisions get made on stale numbers

### 2.4 Debt Covenant Compliance

#### Workflow Steps

1. **Covenant Monitoring Setup**
   - Extract covenant requirements from loan documents
   - Build covenant calculation models
   - Set up automated tracking systems
   - Define monitoring frequency (monthly/quarterly)
   - Establish alert thresholds

2. **Regular Covenant Testing**
   - Calculate DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio)
   - Calculate LTV (Loan-to-Value) ratio
   - Test debt yield requirements
   - Verify net worth and liquidity covenants
   - Check reporting covenant compliance

3. **Lender Reporting**
   - Prepare quarterly/annual compliance certificates
   - Submit required financial statements
   - Provide rent rolls and operating reports
   - Submit property valuations as required
   - Document covenant compliance

4. **Breach Prevention and Management**
   - Monitor covenant headroom
   - Receive automated alerts when approaching thresholds
   - Develop remediation plans for potential breaches
   - Communicate proactively with lenders
   - Cure breaches or request waivers

#### Key Financial Covenants

**Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR):**
- Measures how well property income covers debt payments
- Formula: Net Operating Income / Annual Debt Service
- Lenders typically require DSCR of 1.20-1.40
- A DSCR greater than one means there is sufficient cash flow to cover debt service

**Loan-to-Value Ratio (LTV):**
- Compares loan amount to property value
- Formula: Outstanding Loan Balance / Current Property Value
- Lower LTVs (below 80%) considered safer and lead to better loan terms
- Tested quarterly or annually via appraisals or valuations

**Debt Yield:**
- Measures NOI as percentage of loan amount
- Formula: Net Operating Income / Outstanding Loan Balance
- Common requirement: minimum 7-10% debt yield

**Net Worth and Liquidity:**
- Borrowers often need a net worth equal to the loan amount
- Liquidity requirement typically 10% of the loan value
- Tested annually via personal financial statements

### 2.5 Distribution Calculations

#### Workflow Steps

1. **Calculate Distributable Cash**
   - Start with Net Operating Income
   - Subtract debt service payments
   - Subtract CapEx expenditures
   - Subtract required reserves
   - Subtract property-level fees
   - = Distributable Cash Flow

2. **Apply Waterfall Structure**
   - **Tier 1**: Return of capital + preferred return to LPs
   - **Tier 2**: GP catch-up (if applicable)
   - **Tier 3+**: Profit split per agreed promote structure

3. **Prepare Distribution Analysis**
   - Calculate LP and GP allocations
   - Prepare distribution statements by investor
   - Track cumulative distributions vs. capital contributions
   - Calculate IRR and equity multiple to date
   - Model projected returns to exit

4. **Process Distributions**
   - Obtain distribution approval
   - Execute wire transfers
   - Issue distribution notices to investors
   - Update investor reporting systems
   - Maintain distribution history

#### Distribution Waterfall Structure

A waterfall is a legal term used in an Operating Agreement that describes how money is paid, when it is paid, and to whom it is paid in commercial real estate equity investments. A Real Estate Waterfall is a tier-based model for measuring the proper distribution of proceeds between a general partner (GP) and its limited partners (LPs).

**Key Parties:**
- **General Partner (GP/Sponsor)**: The active manager of an investment fund responsible for deal origination, sourcing potential investments, performing diligence, and managing the properties post-acquisition
- **Limited Partners (LPs)**: The capital providers of the fund, passive investors for whom the GP is investing

**Typical Waterfall Tiers:**

**Tier 1 - Return of Capital + Preferred Return:**
- The first tier distributes cash flow to the partners until the LP has achieved some defined preferred return and received a full return of capital
- The preferred rate usually ranges between 6% to 8% on an annual basis, based on the internal rate of return (IRR) accrued
- 100% to LPs until capital returned plus preferred return

**Tier 2 - GP Catch-Up:**
- The GP catch-up provision allocates distributions to the GP once the fund manager returns contributed capital and reaches the preferred return
- The provision's purpose is to incentivize fund managers to create returns in excess of the preferred return
- Typically 100% to GP until they reach their agreed promote percentage

**Tier 3+ - Promote/Carried Interest:**
- The term "promote" refers to the disproportionate allocation of fund proceeds that flow to the general partner (GP) after reaching the target returns
- Often used interchangeably with carried interest (or "carry"), as a performance-based contingency payment
- The most common carried interest allocates 20% of the profit to the GP
- Example: 80%/20% split (80% to LPs, 20% to GP) or 70%/30% split

**Distribution Example:**

A simple waterfall model may be a sponsor who offers an 8% preferred return and then a 70%/30% split, telling investors that they should expect to receive their pro rata share of the distributable cash flow from a transaction until they have received an 8% return on their investment, after which profits are split 70% to LPs and 30% to GP.

#### European vs American Waterfalls

**European Waterfall:**
- Sponsors are not entitled to receive carried interest until the limited partners (LPs) are made whole (i.e. full recovery of the original capital contribution, often inclusive of a predefined preferred return)
- More conservative and LP-favorable

**American Waterfall:**
- Sponsors are entitled to receive carried interest on a deal-by-deal basis, i.e. individual fund investments before the LPs are "made whole"
- The American waterfall is perceived as more favorable to sponsors

- Operating Agreement with waterfall provisions
- Capital account statements by investor
- Distribution calculation models
- Historical distribution records
- IRR and equity multiple calculations
- Cash flow projections to exit
- Distribution notices and wire confirmations
- K-1 tax reporting data

## 3. REPORTING

### 3.1 Investor Reporting (Quarterly and Annual)

#### 3.1.1 Quarterly Reporting

**Workflow Steps:**

1. **Data Collection and Validation (Days 1-5 after quarter-end)**
   - Close accounting period
   - Reconcile all accounts
   - Obtain property manager reports
   - Validate rent roll accuracy
   - Review covenant compliance
   - Collect property photos and updates

2. **Financial Package Preparation (Days 6-10)**
   - Prepare income statement vs. budget
   - Prepare balance sheet
   - Prepare cash flow statement
   - Calculate key metrics (NOI, DSCR, occupancy, etc.)
   - Prepare variance explanations
   - Update financial forecasts

3. **Narrative and Commentary (Days 11-15)**
   - Write executive summary
   - Document operational highlights
   - Explain financial performance
   - Describe leasing activity
   - Report on capital projects
   - Discuss market conditions
   - Outline next quarter priorities

4. **Review and Distribution (Days 16-20)**
   - Internal review and quality control
   - Senior management approval
   - Format and finalize report
   - Distribute to investors
   - Schedule investor calls if needed

**Report Components:**

Quarterly reports are issued every three months to update investors on a property's or fund's performance, operational activities, and overall financial health:

- Executive summary (1-2 pages)
- Property overview and key metrics dashboard
- Financial statements:
  - Income statement (actual vs. budget)
  - Balance sheet
  - Cash flow summary
- Occupancy and leasing report
- Rent roll summary
- Capital expenditure update
- Market update
- Property photos and news
- Upcoming quarter outlook

#### 3.1.2 Annual Reporting

**Workflow Steps:**

1. **Year-End Close (Weeks 1-2)**
   - Complete full accounting close
   - Process all year-end adjustments
   - Finalize depreciation schedules
   - Conduct physical asset verification
   - Obtain property valuations
   - Engage external auditors if required

2. **Comprehensive Financial Analysis (Weeks 3-4)**
   - Prepare full-year financial statements
   - Calculate annual returns (IRR, equity multiple)
   - Perform multi-year trend analysis
   - Benchmark against projections
   - Assess business plan progress
   - Evaluate portfolio performance

3. **Annual Report Development (Weeks 5-6)**
   - Write comprehensive management discussion
   - Include property valuations
   - Provide market forecasts
   - Detail value creation initiatives
   - Report on ESG metrics
   - Present forward strategy

4. **Compliance and Distribution (Weeks 7-8)**
   - Complete required regulatory filings
   - Prepare K-1 tax packages
   - Distribute annual reports
   - Host annual investor meetings
   - Respond to investor queries

**Report Components:**

Annual reports are issued once per year to provide a comprehensive overview of a fund or property:

- Letter from the asset manager
- Portfolio performance summary
- Full-year financial statements (audited if required)
- Property valuations
- Market performance analysis and outlook
- Detailed property-by-property reviews
- ESG and sustainability report
- Regulatory compliance confirmations
- Forward-looking strategy
- Tax reporting packages (K-1s)

#### Documents & Data

- Monthly/quarterly financial statements
- Rent rolls and lease summaries
- Operating expense detail
- CapEx project reports
- Occupancy and leasing statistics
- Property valuations
- Covenant compliance certificates
- Market research reports
- Property photos and marketing materials
- Investor distribution history
- ESG performance metrics

#### Federal Compliance Requirements (2026)

Compliance in CRE investor reporting has tightened significantly, with new federal and state regulations:

**FinCEN Real Estate Reporting Rule (Effective December 1, 2025):**
- Nationwide reporting of residential property transfers conducted through legal entities or trusts
- Escrow and settlement agents required to file detailed reports
- Must include beneficial ownership information, property details, purchase price, and payment method

**Industry Standards:**
- The CREFC Investor Reporting Package (IRP) is now a must for consistent reporting in CMBS and CLO transactions
- The CRE Finance Council Investor Reporting Package is widely regarded as the benchmark for reporting in commercial mortgage-backed securities and CRE collateralized loan obligation transactions
- Provides standardized templates that promote consistency across the sector

**Additional Requirements:**
- Increased compliance and annual reporting requirements for Qualified Opportunity Funds
- Reporting now includes embodied carbon and biodiversity
- Asset-specific metrics for office, retail, and multifamily properties

### 3.3 ESG/Sustainability Reporting

#### 3.3.1 GRESB Reporting (2026 Standards)

**Major Changes for 2026:**

The 2026 GRESB updates mark a shift toward measurable, performance-based climate action in real estate, emphasizing embodied carbon, credible net-zero targets, climate risk, and stronger collaboration.

**Four Key Changes:**

1. **Embodied Carbon Scoring (Most Significant Change)**
   - Full scoring of embodied carbon introduced
   - New five-point indicator for embodied carbon in the Development component
   - Captures entire life-cycle impact of assets
   - Asset managers must track embodied carbon in new developments and major refurbishments

2. **Tenant Engagement**
   - Scoring weight for key tenant engagement indicators increased from 1.5 points to 2.5 points each
   - Applies to green leases and fit-out/refurbishment programs
   - Emissions from landlord-controlled tenant spaces now classified under Scopes 1 and 2 instead of Scope 3
   - Aligns with GHG Protocol

3. **Net-Zero Target Accountability**
   - Participants must identify which assets within their portfolio are covered by entity-level net-zero targets
   - Increased transparency and accountability
   - Must demonstrate credible pathway to net-zero

4. **Climate Risk Management**
   - Scoring weight for main climate risk management indicators doubled from 2 points to 4 points

### 6.3 Lease Abstraction Accuracy

#### The Problem

Manual lease abstraction traditionally takes four to eight hours per lease, with even experienced professionals requiring at least an hour per document for summarization. Real estate asset managers are still spending an average of 4-8 hours manually abstracting a single commercial lease, and multiplied across a portfolio of thousands of properties, teams are drowning in document review.

**Accuracy Challenges:**

- Manual lease abstraction can result in inaccurate or incomplete information, which can lead to misunderstandings and disputes
- As portfolios grow and lease agreements get more complex, the process becomes increasingly time-consuming, error-prone and frustrating
- Extensive volumes and broad scope and complex terms of commercial leases pose daunting challenges in managing abstraction efficiently

#### AI-Specific Accuracy Concerns

Despite AI adoption, accuracy remains a concern:

- While AI systems boast high accuracy rates, the legal and financial ramifications of errors mean that human oversight is still universally considered vital
- AI output requires thorough human review to catch inaccuracies or misinterpretations of complex or ambiguous clauses
- The phenomenon of AI "hallucinations" remains a concern
- No AI system is flawless, thus prompting the need for manual review or correction to ensure accuracy, particularly in instances involving unusual or ambiguous lease language

#### Legacy Document Challenges

A particularly frustrating challenge is the difficulty AI tools face with older, scanned legacy leases, which may be of poor quality, handwritten in parts, or have inconsistent formatting.

#### Other Major Challenges

- **Multilingual Leases**: Multilingual lease abstraction requires not just linguistic knowledge but also in-depth understanding of lease contracts, and lack of specialized skills results in poorly done abstraction rife with errors and misinterpretation
- **Standards Compliance**: Standards like ASC 842 require meticulous lease data tracking and classification, which increases the need for accurate and timely abstraction
- **Critical Data Points**: Missing or inaccurate abstraction of key terms (rent reviews, break clauses, service charge caps) can have material financial consequences

   - Pain point: Data fragmentation across multiple systems

   - AI opportunity: Real-time monitoring with alerts
   - Critical: Prevent covenant breaches and defaults
   - Requirements: Integration with property financials and debt systems

   - Current state: Manual Excel-based analysis
   - AI opportunity: Automated variance identification and explanation
   - Pain point: Time lag between period-end and insights
   - Solution: Real-time variance alerts with AI-generated explanations
